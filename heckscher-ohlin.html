Project Overview

This page was inspired by the course reading "Resources and Trade: The Heckscher-Ohlin Model," exploring how factor endowments — labor and capital — determine the direction of international trade.

📖

What Inspired This Page

Based on the course reading "Resources and Trade: The Heckscher-Ohlin Model," this page explores how differences in countries' factor endowments (labor and capital) create systematic patterns of international trade through comparative advantage.

⚖️

Main Concept Demonstrated

The Heckscher-Ohlin Theory states that countries export goods whose production uses intensively the factors they possess most abundantly — labor-abundant countries export labor-intensive goods, capital-abundant countries export capital-intensive goods.

🌐

Why This Concept Matters

The H-O model explains global trade patterns, wage inequality between nations, the effects of globalization on income distribution, and why developing countries specialize in manufacturing while developed ones specialize in high-tech and capital-intensive industries.

📊

Real-World Applications

From China's dominance in apparel to Germany's machine exports, from NAFTA debates to trade policy design — the H-O framework provides the theoretical foundation for analyzing virtually every major trade policy question.

💡

"Countries export what uses their abundant resources — the H-O theorem links factor endowments directly to export patterns."

Evidence from Course Work

These panels summarize the key theoretical concepts from the course reading on the Heckscher-Ohlin model, presented as structured economic evidence.

E.01

Heckscher-Ohlin Theorem

The core theorem: "The country abundant in a factor exports the good intensive in that factor." This follows directly from cost differences — when a country has abundant labor, wages are low, making labor-intensive production cheaper and thus competitive in export markets.

🏭
Capital-Abundant → Export Capital-Intensive e.g., Germany exporting machinery, chemicals, aircraft
👕
Labor-Abundant → Export Labor-Intensive e.g., Bangladesh exporting textiles and apparel
E.02

Labor-Intensive vs Capital-Intensive Goods

Type Examples Key Features
Labor-Intensive Clothing, Textiles, Shoes, Furniture High worker hours per unit output
Capital-Intensive Aircraft, Machinery, Chemicals, Autos Heavy equipment investment per worker
Labor-Intensive Food assembly, Electronics assembly Large workforce, modest machinery
Capital-Intensive Semiconductors, Oil refining Highly automated, capital-heavy
E.03

Factor Abundance Definitions

A country is labor-abundant if its labor-to-capital ratio (L/K) is higher than the ratio in the other country. Capital abundance is defined symmetrically.

👥
Labor-Abundant: L/K > L*/K* Lower wage relative to capital cost; cloth production is cheap
⚙️
Capital-Abundant: K/L > K*/L* Lower capital cost relative to wages; food/machinery is cheap
E.04

Factor Price Equalization

Trade in goods acts as an indirect form of factor trade. Under ideal conditions, free trade causes wages and capital returns to converge across countries.

Country A: High Wages
↓ Free Trade Opens ↓
A exports labor-intensive goods
↓ Demand for Labor Equalizes ↓
Wages converge across countries
E.05

Income Distribution Effects

Trade produces winners and losers. The H-O model predicts that owners of abundant factors gain from trade, while owners of scarce factors lose.

✅ Winners
  • Owners of abundant factors
  • Workers in labor-rich countries
  • Capital owners in capital-rich countries
  • Export-sector firms
❌ Losers
  • Owners of scarce factors
  • Workers in capital-rich countries
  • Capital in labor-rich countries
  • Import-competing industries
E.06

The Rybczynski Effect

When the supply of one factor (e.g., labor) increases at constant prices, the output of the labor-intensive sector expands greatly while the capital-intensive sector actually contracts.

Cloth (Labor-Intensive)
+70%
Food (Capital-Intensive)
-25%

Example: After a 10% increase in labor supply, cloth output rises by ~70% while food output contracts by ~25% (at constant factor prices).

Reflection

  • Surprising: Trade's distributional effects — that free trade can actually harm workers in wealthy nations — challenged my initial assumptions that trade is universally beneficial.
  • Challenging: Reconciling the Leontief Paradox (U.S. exports being labor-intensive) with H-O predictions required understanding model limitations.
  • Real-World: The H-O model explains global manufacturing shifts — why apparel moved to Bangladesh, why Germany dominates machinery exports.
  • Policy Link: Trade policy debates (tariffs, quotas) often hinge on exactly which factors of production each country has in abundance.
