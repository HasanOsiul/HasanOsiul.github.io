Section 01 — Project Overview

What Is the Pizza Optimizer?

A classroom activity that transforms a relatable decision — choosing pizza toppings — into a formal optimization problem using Management Science methods.

🎯

Decision Variables

For each of the 10 toppings, we define a binary variable: 1 if selected, 0 if not. These are the knobs our model can turn to reach the best outcome.

xᵢ ∈ {0, 1} for each topping i
📈

Objective Function

Maximize total satisfaction. The model sums the satisfaction score of every selected topping and attempts to make this number as large as possible.

Maximize Σ (satᵢ × xᵢ)
💰

Budget Constraint

Total cost of selected toppings must not exceed 7.00 woowoos. This is the binding constraint that forces trade-offs between high-satisfaction and high-cost toppings.

Σ (costᵢ × xᵢ) ≤ 7.00 ww
⚙️

Why Optimization Matters

Managers constantly face limited resources. The same model applies to marketing budgets, supply chains, staff scheduling, and product selection — any setting where choices must be made under constraints.

"Good decisions are not about choosing everything you want. They are about choosing the best option within your limits."
— Core Principle, Management Science

Section 04 — What Did I Learn?

Knowledge, Skills & Reflection

Three dimensions of learning from a deceptively simple pizza problem that reveals deep Management Science principles.

🧠

Knowledge

  • Management Science modeling methodology
  • Binary (0/1) decision variables
  • Objective function design
  • Constraint formulation
  • Optimization and feasibility
  • Sensitivity analysis
  • Spreadsheet-based modeling
🛠️

Skills

  • Quantitative reasoning under constraints
  • Spreadsheet analysis and modeling
  • Structured problem-solving
  • Critical thinking about trade-offs
  • Data interpretation and visualization
  • Decision-making with incomplete information
  • Model building from real-world scenarios
💡

Reflection

The most surprising insight: the highest-satisfaction topping (Salame at 8.3) was always in the optimal solution, but the second highest (Parma Ham at 7.2) was never selected — because at 3.00 ww it consumed too much budget for what it offered relative to cheaper alternatives.

A mathematical model evaluated over 1,024 combinations in milliseconds — something no human could do by intuition alone.

Real-world applications: budget planning, marketing mix, supply chain management, product portfolio selection, staff scheduling, and resource allocation in any constrained environment.
