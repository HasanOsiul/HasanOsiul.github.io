⚖️

The Heckscher-Ohlin Theory

Countries export products that intensively use the factors of production they possess abundantly. If a country has abundant labor, it tends to export labor-intensive goods. If it has abundant capital, it tends to export capital-intensive goods.

👷

Labor-Intensive Goods

Goods requiring large amounts of human labor relative to machinery or capital investment.

  • Textiles
  • Footwear
  • Hand-sewn Apparel
  • Call Center Services
🏭

Capital-Intensive Goods

Goods requiring large amounts of machinery, technology, and financial investment.

  • Semiconductors
  • Luxury Watches
  • Steel
  • Smartphones
"Countries export what uses their abundant resources."

📚 Key Definitions CONCEPTUAL

Factor Intensity

The relative amount of labor and capital needed to produce a good

Labor-Intensive

Goods requiring large amounts of labor relative to capital

Capital-Intensive

Goods needing significant machinery, technology, and investment

Factor Endowment

Total amount of labor and capital available within a country

Labor-Abundant

Country with a high L/K ratio — more workers relative to capital

Capital-Abundant

Country with a low L/K ratio — more capital relative to workers

Reflection

Countries do not always trade because they are better at producing something. Trade occurs because countries have different resource endowments — a fascinating insight.

I was surprised by how accurately a simple L/K ratio can predict real-world trade patterns.

Real-world applications: Global manufacturing, international business, trade policy, economic development, and labor markets.
